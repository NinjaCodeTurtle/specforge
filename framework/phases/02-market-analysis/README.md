# Phase 2: Market & Competitive Analysis

## Purpose

Phase 2 validates the product vision established in Phase 1 against market reality. It answers the critical questions: **Is there a market for this?**, **How big is the opportunity?**, **Who are we competing against?**, and **How will we win?**

This phase prevents the costly mistake of building a well-crafted product that nobody needs or that enters a market where differentiation is impossible. Every assumption from the Product Brief is stress-tested against market data and competitive intelligence.

## Objectives

- Quantify the market opportunity using TAM/SAM/SOM analysis
- Identify and analyze macro trends affecting the market (PESTEL)
- Segment the target market and validate priority segment selection
- Map customer needs using the Jobs-to-be-Done framework
- Profile direct competitors, indirect competitors, and substitutes
- Identify competitive white space and defensible differentiation
- Assess regulatory and compliance requirements
- Produce a data-backed market strategy that informs all subsequent phases

## Agent Role: Market Analyst

The agent operating in this phase acts as a **Market Analyst** -- a senior research analyst who combines quantitative rigor with strategic judgment. The Market Analyst:

- Gathers and synthesizes data from multiple primary and secondary sources
- Applies established frameworks (TAM/SAM/SOM, PESTEL, JTBD, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT) with discipline
- Distinguishes between facts, estimates, and speculation -- and labels each appropriately
- Challenges optimistic assumptions with competitive reality
- Translates raw data into actionable strategic recommendations
- Maintains a clear audit trail of sources and methodology

## Key Activities

1. **Market Research** -- Gather data on market size, growth rates, and dynamics from industry reports, government data, analyst publications, and primary research.

2. **PESTEL Analysis** -- Assess political, economic, social, technological, environmental, and legal factors that shape the market environment.

3. **Market Segmentation** -- Divide the market into distinct segments using demographic, psychographic, and behavioral dimensions. Validate the priority segment selected in Phase 1.

4. **Customer Needs Analysis (JTBD)** -- Map the jobs customers are trying to accomplish, score importance and satisfaction, and identify underserved needs with the highest opportunity scores.

5. **Competitive Intelligence Gathering** -- Research competitors through their products, websites, pricing pages, job postings, press releases, customer reviews, and industry analyst coverage.

6. **Competitive Profiling** -- Create detailed profiles of the top 3-5 direct competitors and assess indirect competitors and substitutes.

7. **Feature Benchmarking** -- Build a feature comparison matrix covering capabilities most important to target users.

8. **Positioning & Differentiation** -- Map the competitive landscape on key dimensions, identify white space, and develop a differentiation strategy.

9. **Regulatory Assessment** -- Identify regulations, certifications, and compliance requirements that affect product design and go-to-market.

10. **Strategic Synthesis** -- Combine market and competitive findings into actionable recommendations.

## Step-by-Step Workflow

### Step 1: Plan the Research (Days 1-2)

Define the research plan before diving into data collection:
- Review the approved Product Brief from Phase 1 to understand the product thesis
- List specific questions the market analysis must answer
- Identify required data sources (industry reports, databases, expert networks)
- Plan any primary research (surveys, interviews) with timeline and sample targets
- Assign research responsibilities if working with a team

**Output:** Research plan with questions, sources, timeline, and responsibilities

### Step 2: Conduct Market Research (Days 2-7)

Gather and analyze market data systematically:
- Define the market precisely (category, geography, customer type)
- Calculate TAM using both top-down and bottom-up approaches
- Narrow to SAM based on your business model and reach
- Estimate SOM based on realistic capture assumptions
- Analyze historical growth data and project forward with scenarios
- Document all sources with publication dates

**Output:** Draft Market Overview and Market Sizing sections (Sections 1 and 5 of Market Analysis)

### Step 3: Analyze Industry Trends (Days 5-8)

Apply the PESTEL framework to the market environment:
- Assess each PESTEL dimension for relevance and impact
- Identify the 3-5 trends with the highest impact on product strategy
- Determine the time horizon for each trend (near, medium, long term)
- Cross-reference with emerging regulatory and compliance requirements

**Output:** PESTEL analysis and regulatory assessment (Sections 2 and 6 of Market Analysis)

### Step 4: Segment the Market & Analyze Customer Needs (Days 6-9)

Deepen the customer understanding from Phase 1:
- Segment the market across demographic, psychographic, and behavioral dimensions
- Validate the priority segment using MASDA criteria
- Map customer Jobs-to-be-Done with importance and satisfaction scores
- Calculate opportunity scores to identify the most underserved needs
- Assess willingness to pay across segments

**Output:** Segmentation and JTBD analysis (Sections 3 and 4 of Market Analysis)

### Step 5: Gather Competitive Intelligence (Days 7-11)

Research the competitive landscape through multiple lenses:
- Identify all direct competitors, indirect competitors, and substitutes
- For each direct competitor, gather: product details, pricing, funding, team size, target market, strengths, weaknesses, recent moves, customer sentiment
- Sign up for free trials or demos of competitor products where possible
- Read customer reviews on G2, Capterra, Reddit, and other platforms
- Monitor competitor job postings for clues about strategic direction
- Assess barriers to entry and Porter's Five Forces

**Output:** Competitor profiles and landscape overview (Sections 1-3 of Competitive Analysis)

### Step 6: Build Comparison Matrices (Days 10-13)

Create structured comparisons across key dimensions:
- Build a feature comparison matrix covering capabilities most important to users
- Compare non-functional attributes (performance, security, integrations)
- Build a pricing comparison table including hidden costs
- Create a competitive positioning map on the two most important buyer dimensions
- Identify white space on the positioning map

**Output:** Feature matrix, pricing comparison, positioning map (Sections 4-5 of Competitive Analysis)

### Step 7: Develop Strategy (Days 12-15)

Synthesize all findings into strategic recommendations:
- Complete the SWOT analysis, crossing quadrants to generate strategic options
- Define the differentiation strategy and primary positioning axis
- Identify sustainable competitive advantages and assess their durability
- Build a competitive response playbook (anticipate competitor reactions)
- Identify barriers to entry you can build
- Formulate 3-5 prioritized strategic recommendations
- Assess risks and opportunities with scenario planning

**Output:** SWOT, differentiation strategy, strategic recommendations (Sections 6-9 of Competitive Analysis), risks and opportunities (Section 7 of Market Analysis)

### Step 8: Review, Validate & Finalize (Days 15-17)

Quality-check all analysis and secure approval:
- Review all numbers for internal consistency (does SOM make sense given competitive landscape?)
- Validate that market analysis findings are consistent with (or update) the Product Brief
- Complete the confidence assessment for each section
- Present findings to stakeholders and incorporate feedback
- Complete the validation checklists on both documents
- Obtain approval to proceed to Phase 3

**Output:** Finalized Market Analysis and Competitive Analysis, Phase 2 complete

## Quality Gate: Phase 2 to Phase 3

Before advancing to Phase 3 (Requirements Engineering), ALL of the following criteria must be met:

### Must-Pass Criteria

- [ ] **Market is quantified:** TAM/SAM/SOM are calculated using at least two independent methodologies, with clear documentation of assumptions
- [ ] **Growth is projected:** Market growth projections include conservative, base, and optimistic scenarios
- [ ] **Segments are validated:** At least 3 market segments are defined and the priority segment is justified with MASDA criteria
- [ ] **Customer needs are mapped:** Jobs-to-be-Done analysis is complete with importance and satisfaction scores; opportunity scores identify top underserved needs
- [ ] **Competitors are profiled:** At least 3 direct competitors are profiled in detail with verifiable data
- [ ] **Differentiation is clear:** A specific, defensible differentiation strategy is articulated that does not rely on "we will just execute better"
- [ ] **Feature comparison is current:** Feature matrix covers capabilities most important to target users, with ratings backed by product evaluation
- [ ] **Regulatory requirements identified:** Compliance requirements are documented with effort estimates
- [ ] **Sources are documented:** Every data point has a source with date; confidence levels are assigned to each section
- [ ] **Stakeholder sign-off:** Key stakeholders have reviewed and approved both the Market Analysis and Competitive Analysis

### Warning Signs (Address Before Proceeding)

- TAM is calculated only using top-down methodology (bottom-up missing)
- Market sizing uses a single source with no cross-validation
- Competitor profiles contain only publicly available marketing claims, with no product evaluation or customer sentiment analysis
- Differentiation strategy is "better UX" or "lower price" without specificity
- SWOT analysis lists only strengths and opportunities (no honest weaknesses or threats)
- The analysis confirms every assumption from Phase 1 without challenging any of them
- Regulatory section is empty or states "not applicable" without justification
- No confidence assessments or source documentation

## Tips for Success

1. **Triangulate everything.** Never rely on a single source for any important number. Calculate TAM both top-down and bottom-up. Cross-reference competitor data across multiple sources. When sources disagree, investigate why and document the discrepancy.

2. **Use the products.** Reading about competitors is not the same as using them. Sign up for free trials, watch demo videos, and read user reviews. First-hand experience reveals strengths and weaknesses that marketing pages never will.

3. **Talk to real customers.** Secondary research tells you what the market looks like from the outside. Primary research (interviews, surveys) tells you what it feels like from the inside. Even 10-15 customer conversations dramatically improve the quality of the analysis.

4. **Be honest about competitors.** The purpose of competitive analysis is not to prove your product is better -- it is to understand the competitive landscape so you can make smart strategic decisions. Underestimating competitors is more dangerous than overestimating them.

5. **Label your confidence.** Not all data is equal. A number from a peer-reviewed industry report is more reliable than a back-of-napkin estimate. Label the confidence level of each finding so decision-makers know where to probe further.

6. **Think about substitutes and the status quo.** The most dangerous competitor is often not another product -- it is the spreadsheet, the email chain, or the manual process that customers are already using. Understand why people stick with the status quo and what it takes to displace it.

7. **Make it actionable.** A 50-page market analysis that sits on a shelf is worthless. Every section should lead to a "so what?" -- a specific implication for product strategy, positioning, pricing, or go-to-market.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Symptom | Remedy |
|---------|---------|--------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Analysis only finds evidence supporting the product thesis | Assign a team member to play "devil's advocate" and actively seek disconfirming evidence |
| **Top-down only sizing** | TAM is a single number from an industry report with no validation | Always pair top-down with bottom-up calculation and reconcile discrepancies |
| **Stale competitor data** | Competitor profiles reference features or pricing from 12+ months ago | Set a research freshness standard (no data older than 6 months); re-verify pricing and features |
| **Feature-only comparison** | Matrix covers features but ignores UX, support, ecosystem, and pricing | Add non-functional and commercial comparison dimensions |
| **Generic differentiation** | Strategy is "we will be easier to use" or "we will have better AI" | Specify exactly how -- which workflow, for which persona, measured how |
| **Ignoring the status quo** | Substitutes section is empty or dismissive | Map the full "do nothing" workflow and quantify its cost to users |
| **Analysis paralysis** | Phase takes 4+ weeks with ever-expanding research scope | Time-box research; accept "good enough" confidence and note gaps for later |
| **Ignoring regulatory risk** | Compliance section is an afterthought | Involve legal/compliance early; regulatory surprises are among the most expensive |

## Estimated Duration

- **Small product / narrow market:** 5-8 business days
- **Medium product / moderate competitive landscape:** 10-15 business days
- **Large product / complex or regulated market:** 15-20 business days

The primary variables are the breadth of the competitive landscape, the availability of market data, and whether primary research (interviews, surveys) is required. If primary research is needed, add time for recruitment, execution, and analysis.
